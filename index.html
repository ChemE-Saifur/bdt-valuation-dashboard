4. How to Calculate BDT Overvaluation Yourself

A. Cross-rate calculation

BDT per competitor currency = USD/BDT ÷ USD/competitor currency Example: USD/BDT = 122.62 USD/INR = 94.787 BDT/INR = 122.62 ÷ 94.787 = 1.2936 BDT per INR

B. Inflation-adjusted required exchange rate

Required BDT/competitor rate today = Base BDT/competitor rate × (Bangladesh CPI index today ÷ Bangladesh CPI index at base) ÷ (Competitor CPI index today ÷ Competitor CPI index at base)

C. Overvaluation calculation

Overvaluation (%) = (Required BDT/competitor rate - Actual BDT/competitor rate) ÷ Actual BDT/competitor rate × 100 If the number is positive: BDT is overvalued against that competitor. If the number is negative: BDT is undervalued against that competitor.

D. Why inflation differentials accumulate

Suppose Bangladesh inflation since the base date is 9% and India inflation is 4%. Then Bangladesh's price level has risen roughly 5% more than India's. If BDT does not depreciate by roughly that amount against INR, Bangladesh loses real price competitiveness. This gap accumulates over time.

6. Reproducibility Checklist

  1. Keep the base exchange rates fixed at the base date.
  2. Update current USD/BDT, USD/INR, USD/VND and USD/CNY.
  3. Update cumulative CPI index since the base date for Bangladesh, India, Vietnam and China.
  4. Calculate actual current BDT cross-rates.
  5. Calculate required inflation-adjusted BDT cross-rates.
  6. Compare required rates with actual rates.
  7. If required BDT depreciation is greater than actual depreciation, BDT is overvalued.
Important caution: This dashboard estimates export-competitiveness misalignment, not a trading forecast. Exact valuation requires full trade weights, price indices, base periods, productivity trends, terms of trade, capital flows and reserve policy.

8. Simple Interpretation Rule

If Bangladesh inflation is higher than India, Vietnam or China, but BDT does not depreciate enough against those currencies, then BDT appreciates in real terms. This acts like a hidden tax on Bangladesh exporters because their costs rise faster than competitor costs while the exchange rate does not compensate them.
